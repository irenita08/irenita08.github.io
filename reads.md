Probably not comprehensive, but trying to be better at tracking and writing down learnings and  impressions before I forget them. In rough order of how I read them since starting the sabbatical. 

- *A day in the life of Abed Salama: Anatomy of a Jerusalem Tragedy* by Nathan Thrall - a beautiful, searing book that everyone should read.

- *The Undoing Project* by Michael Lewis - the story of the collaboration between Israeli psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, the minds that gave us behavioral economics.

- *These Truths* by Jill Lepore - gorgeously written broad survey of US history. Now patiently waiting in line for Lepore’s new history of the constitution (*We The People)* to become available on Libby.

- *A Carpet Ride to Khiva: Seven Years on the Silk Road -* Read this before going to Uzbekistan and spending a week by myself in Khiva, and loved it. Beautiful window into an Uzbekistan recently emerging from Soviet times, so quite different from the one I encountered in 2025 (which was nonetheless every bit as fascinating).

- *The Silk Roads: a New History of the World -* should’ve read this a long time ago, but what better book to read while *on* the silk roads. Good reminder that the very center of the world *always* shifts, and just because you happened to be born during the peak of a given empire doesn’t mean the world won’t change under your feet.

- *Benjamin Franklin - An American Life* by Walter Isaacson - Franklin would be my answer to “if you could meet any historical character, who would you pick.” Felt a dash of Penn pride I hadn’t felt in a while at having been formed at the university that was his brainchild.

- *Alexander Hamilton* by Ron Chernow - simply incredible life and story (that much is evident from the musical, but the full story is even more remarkable).

- *The Hemingses of Monticello* by Annette Gordon-Reed - Pulitzer-prize winning history of Thomas Jefferson’s “other” family: the woman he spent most of his life with and had at least six children with, who was his slave, and of her family and children and the world they inhabited.

- *A Short History of Nearly Everything* by Bill Bryson - became one of my favorite books of all time. Reminder of the sheer wonder that is this world and our extremely unlikely existence in it.

- *How to Hide an Empire* by Daniel Immerwahr - essential book about the usually ignored history of the US’ overseas possessions and how the American empire has shifted forms over time.

- *Breakneck* by Dan Wang - there are lots of good books about modern China (the last one I remember reading being the excellent *Age of Ambition* by Evan Osnos), but this is the first one I have read post-pandemic and including first-hand accounts of China’s extreme (to put it mildly) lockdowns. I generally agree with his assertion that both superpowers would be better off borrowing some things from the other, and found the “engineers vs. lawyers” framing both entertaining and insightful.

- *Embracing Defeat: Japan in the Wake of World War II* by John W. Dower - I’ve become fascinated with the topic of how countries recover from near complete devastation, and learning about this process in Japan while traveling around Vietnam reminded me of just how much of the world emerged from literal ashes and unfathomable darkness way more recently than we usually stop to think about.
